The Five Things That Can Cause a Paycut

Each item below is a decision point—or a decision you may be forced into if the risk shows up. The allocator tests how your current choices hold up, with charts and narrative guidance in your report.

  1. 1

    Relying on the Flaw of Averages

    The sequence of returns—not just the average—can determine whether money lasts 24 years or 30+, even with identical withdrawals and the same arithmetic mean.

  2. 2

    Living Too Long

    Outliving your income plan is one of the most common paycuts. Inflation, taxes, and a long retirement can erode purchasing power when predictable income does not keep pace.

  3. 3

    Dying Too Soon

    A premature death can cut income for a surviving spouse or household when assets and protection are not aligned to replace lost earnings and benefits.

  4. 4

    Underestimating Care Costs

    Long-term care is often under-modeled until it is needed. A sustained care event can redirect cash flow away from lifestyle spending—functionally reducing retirement pay.

  5. 5

    Getting Sued

    Liability awards above auto, home, and umbrella coverage can force assets to be used— cutting what your plan can otherwise provide for income and security.

Decisions at Retirement

A successful retirement plan is less about picking one product and more about coordinating decisions made at—and after—the retirement date. These are the kinds of choices the 5 Things analysis and a full planning conversation are designed to clarify.

  • When and how to retire Timing, income start dates, and whether the household can sustain the lifestyle it expects.
  • How to turn assets into income Withdrawal rates, guaranteed vs. market-based income, and the flaw of relying on averages alone.
  • Which risks to insure vs. self-fund Long-term care, survivor needs, liability, and whether assets can absorb a shock without a paycut.
  • Social Security & Medicare Claiming strategies, health coverage, and institutional rules that affect cash flow.
  • Qualified & non-qualified assets Which accounts to draw from, in what order, and with what tax consequences.
  • Real estate & the full balance sheet Whether home equity belongs in the plan—and when it should be used relative to investments.
  • Estate & tax coordination Legacy goals, beneficiary alignment, and keeping more of what the plan produces.
